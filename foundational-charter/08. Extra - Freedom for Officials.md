# Freedom for Officials

Political dissatisfaction should be addressed through dismissal, replacement, public scrutiny, and institutional reform, rather than by turning vague allegations into criminal weapons against former officials.<br/>

Public officials must have the freedom to govern, make decisions, lawfully exercise their authority, and make political mistakes. They should not be punished merely because a succeeding government characterizes their past decisions as corruption or abuse of power.<br/>

Corruption and abuse of power are moral accusations; they do not, by themselves, constitute criminal offenses under the law.<br/>

Embezzlement of public funds is a criminal offense. Any prosecution for embezzlement of public funds must be initiated within three months after the alleged act occurred. Once that period has expired, the courts shall no longer accept or adjudicate such cases.<br/>

Any crime committed by a public official through the exercise of official power or the implementation of a policy must be prosecuted within three months. After this limitation period expires, the courts shall no longer accept the case.<br/>

For example, suppose Police Minister A orders Police Officer B to assault Citizen C. Within three months, Police Minister A may be prosecuted for causing harm to Citizen C. Once the three-month period has expired, Police Minister A may no longer be prosecuted for that act.<br/>

Police Officer B’s assault on Citizen C, however, constitutes direct harm and is not subject to any time limit for prosecution. The court shall determine whether Police Officer B should be exempted from criminal liability.<br/>
