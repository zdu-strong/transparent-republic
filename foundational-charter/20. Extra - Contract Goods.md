
# Contract goods

## Contract Eggs

The government signs contracts with egg companies that specify the subsidy amount, the minimum quantity of eggs to be supplied, the maximum egg price, quality standards.<br/>

If an egg company violates the contract, the government may refuse to renew it.<br/>

The government may sign contracts with multiple egg companies, and the subsidy amount may differ from one company to another.<br/>

Egg companies that do not sign a contract are still free to sell eggs.<br/>

Egg companies are allowed to monopolize the market, with egg products exempted from antitrust laws.<br/>

During a crisis, several egg companies breach their contracts and raise their prices. The government terminates its contract with only one of them and increases the subsidies provided to the remaining companies.<br/>

During a crisis, if essential goods are in short supply, the government can raise the price ceiling for contract eggs while also increasing basic living allowance payments.<br/>

For example:<br/>
The production cost of each egg is $0.80, while the market price is $1.40. The government signs a contract with the company and provides a subsidy of $4 million at the beginning of each month. Under the contract, the company must supply at least 10 million eggs per month, and the maximum price for each egg is set at $1.<br/>
The company may breach or terminate the contract at any time without any penalty. The company remains free to do so, although the government may choose not to renew the contract.<br/>
During a crisis, the government raises the maximum price of contract eggs to $5 and provides the company with a $30 million grant to expand egg production. Even if the company later breaches the contract, the grant still belongs to the company.<br/>

Contract eggs are not a privilege granted to egg companies, but a public bargain: subsidy in exchange for price restraint, quality discipline, and supply reliability.<br/>
